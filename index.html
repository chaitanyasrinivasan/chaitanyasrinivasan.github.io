Chaitanya Srinivasan

I am a Master's student at Carnegie Mellon University School of Computer Science studying Automated Science. I completed my undergraduate also at Carnegie Mellon in Computational Biology. My research advisor is Dr. Andreas Pfenning.

Prior to beginning research with the Computational Biology Department, I interned at UPMC Children's Hospital of Pittsburgh in the Pediatric Gastroenterology Department. I studied the metabolomics and lipidomics of asparaginase-associated acute pancreatitis from a clinical perspective under the mentorship of Dr. Sohail Husain.

Research

I am broadly interested in designing computational models to study the genomic basis of neurological disorders. Currently, I am developing machine learning models to systematically design retinal cell type-specific probes that can be targeted to develop therapies for age-related macular degeneration.

Addiction-associated genetic variants implicate cell type- and region-specific cis-regulatory elements in addiction neurobiology (in progress)
Chaitanya Srinivasan*, BaDoi N. Phan*, Alyssa J. Lawler, Easwaran Ramamurthy, Michael Kleyman, Ashley R. Brown, Irene M. Kaplow, Morgan E. Wirthlin, Andreas R. Pfenning
NIDA Genetic Consortium Abstract

We present a framework that maps addiction-associated genetic variation to brain region- and cell type-specific putative cis-regulatory enhancers across human and mouse epigenomic profiles. We further attribute cell-type specific heritability of addiction genetic risk and predict the functional impact of addiction-associated genetic variants using machine learning models.

Transcriptional alterations in opioid use disorder reveal an interplay between neuroinflammation and synaptic remodeling (in review)
Marianne L. Seney, Sam Moon-Kim, Jill R. Glausier, Mariah A. Hildebrand, Xiangning Xue, Wei Zong, Jeibiao Wang, Micah A. Shelton, BaDoi N. Phan, Chaitanya Srinivasan, Andreas R. Pfenning, George C. Tseng, David A. Lewis, Zachary Freyberg, Ryan W. Logan
bioRxiv, 2020.

We conducted the largest transcriptomics study to date using postmortem brains from subjects with OUD. We reveal new connections between the brain's immune system and opioid dependence in the dorsolateral prefrontal cortex and nucleus accumbens.
